# Divorce in Virginia

Divorce is a complex legal process that affects nearly every aspect of your life. This guide covers the major areas of concern when getting divorced in Virginia to help you understand what to expect and how to protect your interests.

## Grounds for Divorce {#grounds-for-divorce}

Virginia recognizes both **fault-based** and **no-fault** grounds for divorce.

### No-Fault Grounds
- **Separation** — The most common ground. Couples must live separately and apart for:
  - **6 months** if they have a signed separation agreement and no minor children
  - **1 year** in all other cases

### Fault-Based Grounds
- Adultery
- Cruelty or reasonable apprehension of bodily harm
- Willful desertion or abandonment (requires 1 year)
- Conviction of a felony and imprisonment

Fault grounds can impact alimony and property division, but they also require additional proof and typically increase litigation costs.

## Residency Requirements {#residency-requirements}

To file for divorce in Virginia, at least one spouse must have been a **bona fide resident and domiciliary of Virginia for at least 6 months** before filing. The divorce may be filed in the circuit court of the county or city where either spouse resides.

## Types of Divorce {#types-of-divorce}

### Uncontested Divorce
Both spouses agree on all terms (property, custody, support). This is faster, less expensive, and can often be handled with minimal court involvement once the separation period is complete.

### Contested Divorce
Spouses cannot agree on one or more issues. The court decides unresolved matters after hearings or trial. This process is longer and more costly.

### Divorce from Bed and Board (Limited Divorce)
A legal separation that does not fully dissolve the marriage but establishes separate living arrangements and can address support and custody. Rarely used today.

## The Divorce Process {#the-divorce-process}

### Step-by-Step Overview

1. **Separation** — Begin the required separation period and document your separate living arrangements
2. **File a Complaint** — File a Complaint for Divorce in the Circuit Court of the appropriate jurisdiction
3. **Serve the Defendant** — The other spouse must be formally served with the divorce papers
4. **Response** — The defendant has 21 days (or 60 days if served out of state) to respond
5. **Pendente Lite Motions** — Either party may file motions for temporary relief (*pendente lite*) to address immediate needs while the case is pending, such as temporary spousal support, temporary child custody and visitation, temporary child support, and exclusive use of the marital home
6. **Discovery** — Exchange financial information, documents, and other relevant evidence through interrogatories, requests for production, depositions, and subpoenas
7. **Motions to Compel** — If a party fails to respond to discovery requests or provides incomplete responses, the requesting party may file a Motion to Compel, asking the court to order compliance; sanctions may be imposed for non-compliance
8. **Negotiation / Mediation** — Many issues are resolved through negotiation or court-ordered mediation
9. **Final Decree** — If uncontested, a judge may grant the divorce on paper. If contested, a trial is held and the judge issues a final order

## Child Custody and Visitation {#child-custody-and-visitation}

Virginia courts decide custody based on the **best interests of the child** under Virginia Code § 20-124.3.

### Types of Custody
- **Legal custody** — The right to make major decisions for the child (education, healthcare, religion)
  - *Sole legal custody* — One parent decides
  - *Joint legal custody* — Both parents share decision-making
- **Physical custody** — Where the child lives
  - *Primary physical custody* — Child lives mainly with one parent
  - *Shared physical custody* — Child spends significant time with both parents (often 90+ days each per year)

### Factors the Court Considers
- Age and developmental needs of the child
- Each parent's relationship with the child
- Each parent's ability to cooperate and support the child's relationship with the other parent
- Any history of family abuse or neglect
- The child's reasonable preference (especially for older children)
- Geographic proximity of the parents' homes

Virginia strongly encourages co-parenting and parenting plans that keep both parents involved in the child's life.

## Child Support {#child-support}

Virginia uses **guidelines-based child support** under Virginia Code § 20-108.2. Support is calculated using a formula based on:

- Both parents' gross incomes
- Number of children
- Custody arrangement (sole vs. shared physical custody)
- Work-related childcare costs
- Health insurance premiums for the child
- Other extraordinary expenses (special needs, private school if previously agreed)

### Key Points
- Child support is presumed to follow the guidelines; a judge can deviate with written justification
- Support typically continues until the child turns **18** (or 19 if still in high school)
- Either parent can seek modification if there is a material change in circumstances

## Property Division (Equitable Distribution) {#property-division}

Virginia follows the **equitable distribution** model under Virginia Code § 20-107.3. This means marital property is divided **fairly**, though not necessarily equally.

### Key Concepts
- **Marital property** — Assets and debts acquired during the marriage (regardless of whose name is on them)
- **Separate property** — Assets owned before marriage, or received as gifts or inheritance during marriage
- **Hybrid property** — Property that is part marital and part separate (e.g., a home purchased before marriage but paid down with marital funds)

### Factors the Court Considers
- Length of the marriage
- Each spouse's contributions (financial and non-financial)
- Each spouse's debts and liabilities
- How and when specific assets were acquired
- Tax consequences of the division
- Any use of marital funds to benefit separate property

Common marital assets subject to division include the family home, retirement accounts (401k, pension, IRA), bank accounts, investment accounts, and businesses.

## Spousal Support (Alimony) {#spousal-support}

Virginia courts may award **spousal support** under Virginia Code § 20-107.1. There is no formula — the court has broad discretion.

### Types of Spousal Support
- **Temporary support** — Paid during the pendency of divorce proceedings
- **Rehabilitative support** — Time-limited support to allow a spouse to become self-sufficient
- **Permanent support** — Ongoing support, typically in long marriages where one spouse cannot become self-supporting

### Factors Considered
- Duration of the marriage
- Each spouse's income, earning capacity, and employability
- Standard of living during the marriage
- Contributions of each spouse (including homemaking and child-rearing)
- Age and physical/mental condition of each spouse
- **Fault** — Adultery by the receiving spouse can bar an alimony award entirely

Spousal support typically ends upon the recipient's remarriage or cohabitation with a new partner in a marriage-like relationship.

## Separation Agreements {#separation-agreements}

A **Property Settlement Agreement (PSA)** or **Separation Agreement** is a legally binding contract between spouses that resolves all or most issues before the divorce is finalized. It typically covers:

- Division of marital assets and debts
- Spousal support terms
- Child custody and visitation schedules
- Child support
- Tax filing status and dependency exemptions

A well-drafted agreement, signed by both parties and acknowledged before a notary, can be incorporated into the final divorce decree and become a court order.

## Legal Representation {#legal-representation}

While Virginia allows individuals to represent themselves (*pro se*), divorce involves complex legal and financial issues. Consider consulting a licensed Virginia family law attorney if:

- There are significant assets or debts
- Minor children are involved
- One spouse has significantly higher income or earning potential
- There are concerns about hidden assets
- There is a history of domestic violence or abuse

Many attorneys offer initial consultations. Legal aid organizations may also be able to assist those who qualify based on income.
