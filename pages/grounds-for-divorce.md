# Grounds for Divorce in Virginia

Virginia law requires that a spouse filing for divorce establish legal **grounds** — a recognized legal basis — for ending the marriage. Virginia Code § 20-91 governs divorce from the bond of matrimony and provides for both **no-fault** and **fault-based** grounds.

The choice of grounds has real consequences: fault grounds can affect spousal support, property division, and attorney's fees, but they also require evidence and typically increase the cost and duration of litigation.

## No-Fault Divorce: Separation

The most common path to divorce in Virginia is **no-fault divorce based on separation**. There is no need to prove wrongdoing — only that the parties have lived separately and apart for the required period with at least one spouse intending the separation to be permanent.

### What Counts as Separation

Separation in Virginia is more than just a feeling — it has a legal definition that courts take seriously.

**Living separately and apart** means:
- The parties are **not cohabitating** — they are not sharing a bed, living as a couple, or presenting themselves to the community as married
- At least one spouse has **formed the intent to end the marriage** and has communicated that intent (explicitly or through conduct)
- The separation must be **continuous** — it cannot be interrupted by periods of reconciliation without resetting the clock

**Can spouses separate while living in the same house?**

Yes, but it is difficult to establish and easy to dispute. Courts look for objective evidence of separate lives: sleeping in separate rooms, not sharing meals, not attending social events together, maintaining separate finances, and witnesses who can confirm the arrangement. If the separation is contested, in-home separation claims face significant skepticism.

**What can reset the separation clock?**

Reconciliation — resuming the marital relationship — restarts the separation period from zero. A single act of sexual intercourse is generally considered a resumption of the marriage and resets the clock. Merely attempting reconciliation without success (e.g., attending counseling) does not necessarily reset it, but any genuine resumption of cohabitation does.

### 6-Month Separation

Virginia Code § 20-91(A)(9)(a) allows divorce after a **6-month separation** when **both** of the following conditions are met:

1. **The parties have no minor children** (children who are 18 but still in high school do not qualify)
2. **The parties have a signed, written separation agreement** that resolves all issues (property, support, etc.)

The separation agreement must be:
- Signed by both parties
- Acknowledged before a notary public
- Comprehensive — it must address marital property and debts; if spousal support is waived, that waiver should be explicit

If both conditions are satisfied, the divorce can proceed after just six months of continuous separation, significantly shortening the process.

---

### 1-Year Separation

Virginia Code § 20-91(A)(9)(b) provides for divorce after a **1-year separation** for all cases that do not qualify for the 6-month track — including:

- Couples with **minor children**, regardless of whether a separation agreement exists
- Couples **without a signed separation agreement**, regardless of whether they have children
- Any situation where the 6-month criteria are not fully met

The 1-year period is measured from the date of separation, not the date of filing. A complaint may be filed before the year is up, but the divorce cannot be finalized until the full year has elapsed.

---

### Factors That Affect the Separation Period

Several circumstances can complicate or extend the separation timeline:

**Disputed separation date**
If the parties disagree about when separation began, the court will determine the date based on evidence. The party claiming the earlier date bears the burden of proof. Text messages, emails, witness testimony, and financial records (separate accounts, separate addresses) are commonly used.

**Children**
The presence of minor children is the primary factor that prevents use of the 6-month track. Age is assessed at the time of filing — if children turn 18 before filing and are no longer in high school, the 6-month option may become available.

**No separation agreement**
Even couples without children must have a fully executed separation agreement to use the 6-month track. A partial agreement (e.g., property resolved but support unresolved) does not qualify.

**Fault conduct during separation**
Although the divorce itself is no-fault, conduct that occurs during the separation period — such as adultery — can still be raised as a fault ground and may affect alimony and other issues even in an otherwise no-fault case.

**Geographic separation**
One spouse living out of state does not affect the length of the required separation, but it may affect which state has jurisdiction and which state's law applies. Virginia still requires 6 months of residency by at least one spouse before filing.

## Fault-Based Grounds

Fault-based divorce requires proving specific misconduct by the other spouse. The burden of proof is on the party asserting fault. Virginia courts require **corroboration** — the testimony of the spouse alone is not sufficient; there must be independent evidence or a corroborating witness for most fault grounds.

Fault grounds can:
- **Bar or reduce spousal support** for the at-fault spouse (adultery can completely bar alimony)
- **Influence equitable distribution** — courts may consider the economic impact of the fault
- **Allow divorce without waiting** for a separation period in some cases (though in practice the case still takes time to litigate)

---

### Adultery

**Virginia Code § 20-91(A)(1)**

Adultery is voluntary sexual intercourse by a married person with someone other than their spouse. It is the most consequential fault ground in Virginia divorce law.

**What must be proven:**
- The act of sexual intercourse (or deviant sexual intercourse) occurred
- The spouse had the opportunity and disposition to commit the act

Courts accept circumstantial evidence — direct proof of the act itself is rarely required. Evidence of opportunity (hotel records, phone records, GPS data) combined with evidence of disposition (romantic messages, witness testimony) is typically sufficient.

**Corroboration requirement:**
A spouse cannot prove adultery solely through their own testimony. At least one corroborating piece of evidence or witness is required.

**The impact on alimony:**
Under Virginia Code § 20-107.1(B), a court **shall not** award spousal support to a spouse who committed adultery, unless doing so would constitute a "manifest injustice" based on the parties' relative economic circumstances and the duration of the marriage. This is a high bar — adultery is one of the few absolute bars to alimony in Virginia.

**Statute of limitations:**
There is a 5-year statute of limitations on adultery as a divorce ground. Additionally, **condonation** — knowingly forgiving the adultery and resuming the marital relationship — bars the use of adultery as a ground for divorce.

**Criminal history:**
Adultery remains a Class 4 misdemeanor in Virginia (Virginia Code § 18.2-365), though prosecution is extremely rare.

### Cruelty and Reasonable Apprehension of Bodily Harm

**Virginia Code § 20-91(A)(6)**

This ground covers physical violence and conduct that creates a well-founded fear of physical harm. It does not require that actual violence occur — a pattern of threatening or abusive behavior that causes a spouse to reasonably fear for their safety is sufficient.

**What cruelty means in Virginia:**
- Physical abuse — hitting, striking, or injuring a spouse
- A course of conduct so severe and persistent as to render the marriage unsafe and intolerable
- Actions that endanger the life, limb, or health of the other spouse

**What it does not cover:**
- General incompatibility, rudeness, or emotional unkindness without physical threat
- Isolated minor altercations without a pattern of danger
- Mental or emotional abuse alone (though this may support other relief)

**Reasonable apprehension:**
The apprehension of harm must be **objectively reasonable** — not merely subjective fear. Courts look at the history of the relationship, prior incidents, and whether a reasonable person in the same circumstances would genuinely fear bodily harm.

**Practical use:**
This ground is often alleged in conjunction with a request for a protective order or exclusive use of the marital home. It can support emergency pendente lite relief even before the divorce is resolved.

### Willful Desertion or Abandonment

**Virginia Code § 20-91(A)(6)**

Desertion occurs when one spouse **unjustifiably ends cohabitation** without the consent of the other spouse and **without intent to return**, for a period of at least **one year**.

**Elements required:**
1. One spouse broke off cohabitation
2. The deserting spouse had no justification (e.g., the other spouse's cruelty is a defense)
3. The deserting spouse intended the separation to be permanent or indefinite
4. The other spouse did not consent to the separation
5. The separation has continued for at least one year

**Constructive desertion:**
A spouse who is forced to leave the marital home due to the other spouse's cruelty or misconduct may claim **constructive desertion** — meaning the spouse who drove the other away is legally the "deserter," even though they physically remained in the home. This is a significant doctrine that can shift fault even when the facts seem reversed on the surface.

**Refusal of marital relations:**
Persistent, unjustified refusal of sexual relations (without the other spouse's consent) has historically been recognized as a form of desertion in Virginia, though this is less commonly litigated today.

**Effect on alimony:**
Desertion by the spouse seeking alimony is a factor courts consider and can reduce or eliminate support, though it does not operate as an absolute bar the way adultery does.

### Conviction of a Felony

**Virginia Code § 20-91(A)(3)**

A spouse may seek divorce on the ground that the other spouse has been **convicted of a felony**, sentenced to **confinement for more than one year**, and is **actually confined** at the time of the divorce filing — provided the innocent spouse did not resume cohabitation after learning of the conviction.

**Requirements:**
- The conviction must be for a felony (not a misdemeanor)
- The sentence imposed must exceed one year of confinement
- The convicted spouse must be serving that sentence (not on probation or parole)
- The filing spouse must not have condoned the conviction by resuming the marriage afterward

**Practical considerations:**
This ground is less commonly used because service of process on an incarcerated spouse and navigating property and support issues with a confined party can be complex. However, it does allow the innocent spouse to proceed without waiting for a separation period.

## Divorce from Bed and Board

**Virginia Code § 20-95**

Virginia also recognizes **divorce from bed and board** (*a mensa et thoro*), which is a legal separation that does not dissolve the marriage. The parties remain legally married but are permitted to live separately, and the court can address custody, support, and property.

Grounds for divorce from bed and board include cruelty, reasonable apprehension of bodily harm, willful desertion, and abandonment.

This form of divorce is rarely sought today — most parties pursue a full divorce. However, it may be useful in limited circumstances, such as when a spouse needs the benefits of marriage to continue (e.g., health insurance, military benefits) or when religious beliefs prevent a full divorce.

## Strategic Considerations

**When to pursue fault grounds:**

Fault grounds add complexity and cost, but may be worth pursuing when:
- The other spouse committed adultery and you want to protect against an alimony claim
- There is a history of violence or cruelty that supports immediate emergency relief
- The fault conduct had a significant economic impact on the marital estate (e.g., dissipation of assets)

**When no-fault separation is the better path:**

In most cases, waiting out the separation period and proceeding on no-fault grounds is simpler, less expensive, and less contentious — particularly when children are involved. The damage that contested fault litigation can do to a co-parenting relationship is a serious practical consideration.

**Evidence preservation:**

If you believe fault grounds may be relevant to your case, begin preserving evidence early: save communications, document incidents with dates and details, and consult an attorney before taking any action that could constitute condonation (forgiving the fault) or that could affect your own standing.
